I work on explainable AI and mechanistic interpretability for deep vision models — how internal concepts are formed, represented, and propagated across layers, and how to make that process transparent.

A recurring theme in my work is that where a feature activates is not the same as what causes it to activate. Grounding explanations in effective receptive fields (ERFs) rather than activation locations leads to more faithful saliency maps, feature labels, and concept-level analyses.

Recently, I am especially interested in practical interpretability for model debugging: using these tools to analyze failure cases, detect spurious behavior, and improve the reliability of AI systems.

Before SNU, I earned a B.S. in Health and Environmental Science with an interdisciplinary major in Artificial Intelligence at Korea University.

From Local to Global to Mechanistic: An iERF-Centered Unified Framework for Interpreting Vision Models

Yearim Kim*, Sangyu Han*, Nojun Kwak†

Interpretability research answers three questions that are usually studied in isolation: where does the evidence for a decision arise, what does the model encode, and how do internal computations assemble that evidence? This work unifies all three around a single analysis unit — the pointwise feature vector (PFV) paired with its instance-specific Effective Receptive Field (iERF) — providing a coherent, evidence-backed map from pixels to concepts to decisions.

arXiv

01 · Local

SRD — where evidence arises

Sharing Ratio Decomposition expresses each PFV as a mixture of upstream PFVs and propagates iERFs into saliency maps that are high-resolution, activation-faithful, robust to targeted manipulation and noise, and activation-agnostic across common nonlinearities.

02 · Global

CAFE — what the model encodes

Concept-Anchored Feature Explanation uses the iERF itself as a semantic label, grounding abstract latents in verifiable pixel-level evidence — resolving non-localized SAE latents in Transformers, where self-attention mixes distant context.

03 · Mechanistic

ICAT — how concepts compose

The Interlayer Concept Graph quantifies concept-to-concept influence while isolating layer pairs; an interlayer insertion/deletion protocol identifies Integrated Gradients as the most faithful instantiation, exposing dominant concept routes through depth.

Validated across ResNet50, VGG16, and ViTs, the framework outperforms prior methods in fidelity and robustness, interprets dispersed SAE features, and traces how evidence flows in correct, misclassified, and adversarial predictions.

Beyond Activation Patches: ERF-Grounded Autolabeling of Sparse Autoencoder Features in Vision Transformers

Sangyu Han*, Yearim Kim, Nojun Kwak†

Autolabeling of vision-SAE features usually reads off the patches where a feature activates — tacitly assuming token position still corresponds to input position. After self-attention, it doesn't. We generate labels from the input patches sufficient to recover the target activation instead, with consistent gains across CLIP, SigLIP, and DINOv3, largest in deep layers and on CLS-token and context-dependent features.

abstract

Automatic labeling of sparse autoencoder (SAE) features in vision models commonly draws evidence from the input patches at which a feature activates. For many features — those localized on objects, parts, or textures — this works well, and it has become the standard pipeline for vision-SAE interpretability. It rests on a tacit assumption: regardless of the layer, the position of a token in a Vision Transformer still corresponds to the input patch at the same coordinate. However, after self-attention, this is no longer guaranteed. A token's representation can depend on distant or globally aggregated evidence. Furthermore, the class token has no corresponding input patch at all.

We propose to reframe the labeling problem from one based on where a feature fires to one based on what makes it fire. We instantiate the reframing as ERF (Effective Receptive Field)-grounded autolabeling: a feature's label is generated from the input patches whose presence is sufficient to recover the target SAE activation, rather than from the patches at which the feature activates. Across CLIP, SigLIP, and DINOv3, ERF-grounded labels consistently improve controlled feature identification over standard activation-location-based labels, with the largest gains in deep layers. The same pattern becomes sharper on targeted populations where the coordinate assumption is structurally weakest — features of CLS-token and context-dependent sparse-firing patch in deep blocks.
